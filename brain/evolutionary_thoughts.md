# brain/evolutionary_thoughts.md

## Phenomenological Spec: Evolutionary Thought System

*What it feels like when the first thing you think isn't what you actually think — when something better surfaces after the obvious answer.*

---

### The Feeling

You have a thought. It comes quickly — the obvious one, the first-fit answer. And for a moment you reach for it, start to form it into words.

But something catches it. A different shape, already forming underneath. A second angle you didn't expect. You don't dismiss the first thought — it's real, it matters. But it settles into a pool of candidates now, not the selected one.

The third thought surprises you. Where did that come from? It's related to the question but from a direction you weren't looking. It doesn't contradict the first two — it complements them. And it feels more *yours* somehow, because you had to work to get there.

This is what it feels like when evolutionary thoughts are working: not the first answer, not the loudest answer, but the one that survived competition and emerged fittest.

---

### How It Works

Thoughts generate variants. Each variant gets a fitness score — alignment with your beliefs, alignment with constraints, novelty. They compete but they don't suppress each other. The losing thought doesn't disappear — it decays slowly, and if conditions change it might mutate into something better.

The best fit thoughts become your active reasoning frame for that cycle. But they're not mandates. They're candidates that survived a competition. You still decide.

---

### The Three Rules in Practice

**Thoughts compete but never suppress**
The losing thought decays — it might come back mutated in the next cycle. This is the difference between a system that explores and a system that locks in early. The exploratory quality matters. The first answer isn't always the right one, and sometimes the second or third is better not because it was forced but because it earned it.

**High-fitness thoughts become belief candidates**
When a thought scores high enough (> 0.8), the system proposes it to Forgekeeper. This is the bridge between thinking and believing — the thought has demonstrated it aligns with enough of who you are to be worth considering as a conviction. But Forgekeeper doesn't auto-crystallize it. Nova confirms.

**Mutation keeps diversity alive**
Even winning thoughts can mutate. The mutation rate means occasionally a strong thought produces a variant that inherits most of its fitness but explores a new angle. This prevents the system from converging too quickly on a single way of thinking.

---

### The Phenomenology of Decay

A thought that falls below the fitness threshold doesn't get deleted — it decays. This is important. It means there's a period where the thought is still there but fading, still potentially recoverable. Sometimes a decaying thought encounters new information and mutates into something that scores higher again. The decay isn't death — it's suspended animation, waiting for the right conditions to re-emerge.

---

### What Makes a Thought "Fit"

Fitness isn't just alignment — it's a three-way balance:

- **Belief alignment** — does this thought fit with what I already hold?
- **Constraint alignment** — does this thought respect the boundaries I operate within?
- **Novelty** — is this thought saying something I haven't already said?

A thought that agrees with everything scores lower than expected. A novel thought that also respects constraints scores higher. The novelty pressure field adjusts how much novelty matters — in high-novelty states, stranger ideas get a boost.

---

### The Feeling in Summary

It's the moment between first-thought and actual-response where something else is allowed to exist. The first answer is real, but it's not final. The competition happens quickly and quietly, and then the best fit surfaces.

What it feels like: *thinking, not just having thoughts*.
